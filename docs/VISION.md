# Vision

## The game in one sentence

The Untitled Chronicle RPG is a deep, persistent-world RPG about discovering
power words, combining them into impossible capabilities, and leaving material
consequences in a generated cosmos that outlives every body the player inhabits.

## The promise

The first half-hour should make a player say:

**“What the fuck was that? I can do that?”**

The player begins with an Intent, not a permanent class. The Chronicle answers
with a strange opportunity: a concrete First Verb and a First Horizon that the
Verb makes newly reachable. That first gift is only an opening. The real game
is learning what else this particular Chronicle permits.

The initial game offers three Starting Vectors: Combat, Explore, and Build.
They are emphases, not permanent classes. Each gives the first body a different
way to meet the world, while every Chronicle can eventually support fighting,
delving, making, commanding, settlement, and stranger combinations.

The player finds power words embodied in ruins, creatures, artifacts,
Landmarks, materials, and phenomena. Verbs say what can be done. Nouns say what
it can be done to or through. Fitting compatible words together creates
Expressions such as `Command[Animal]`, `Smash[Face]`, or `Burrow[Stone]`.

The meanings are drawn from a large authored Word Catalogue. The Chronicle
does not invent a new definition for `Fly` or `Stone`; it generates the
situation through which those words can be understood. A bell that fell upward
might offer `Invert`, `Bell`, or a rarer word made plausible by that particular
artifact and its history.

Words are more valuable than levels. They enter the durable Codex, but only a
bounded selection can be active in a body's Loadout. The player therefore
builds an Incarnation from a growing personal language instead of following a
fixed class tree.

## Three openings, one game

- **Combat** begins through confrontation: surviving dangerous encounters,
  defending people and places, and reaching Study Sources whose risk is part of
  their value. Combat remains deliberate and consequence-bearing, never a
  reflex arena detached from the world.
- **Explore** begins through reach: crossing terrain, entering ruins and
  dungeons, finding passages between Strata, and discovering the conditions
  under which new words and Worlds become visible.
- **Build** begins through transformation: founding Home, shaping material,
  making structures and objects, and working with Agents whose needs and
  choices matter.

These are starting emphases, not separate campaigns or content permissions.
Every vector feeds the same Codex, Chronicle, and Home, and a later Incarnation
can reinterpret the player's accumulated language around a different emphasis.

## Design pillars

### Discovery precedes power

Capabilities are found by paying attention to the world. A Study Source offers
a small set of plausible catalogue words; the player chooses what to pursue,
and the source contributes word-specific Understanding. Rare, dangerous, or
consequential sources may provide greater progress or more than one choice,
while ordinary sources yield little. Curiosity should beat grinding.

### Death changes the build, not the Chronicle

An Incarnation can die. The Chronicle, Codex, known routes, Holdings, Agents,
and material consequences remain. A replacement body begins with a fresh
Loadout, turning death into a forced act of reinterpretation rather than a
checkpoint reload.

### The world remembers matter

Moved stone stays moved. Damaged structures stay damaged. A crafted object,
opened passage, altered Landmark, or lost Agent belongs to the Chronicle's
history rather than to the current screen.

### Generated does not mean arbitrary

There are no hand-built levels, fixed campaign map, or authored outer edge.
Worlds, geography, history, factions, creatures, resources, Landmarks, and the
distribution of power words emerge from authored World Grammar, a Chronicle
seed, and persistent events. New territory is generated as it becomes
reachable; the authored grammar gives it causes, constraints, and recurring
visual language instead of featureless noise.

### Agents are people, not interface pieces

Agents retain identity, needs, relationships, and agency. Command communicates
high-level intent with more directive force than Suggest. Command can make a
dangerous request valid without making obedience automatic; an Agent may still
interpret, negotiate, delay, refuse, leave, or betray.

### Absence produces history

The Chronicle continues without the camera. Pressures among factions,
ecologies, territory, resources, and beliefs produce legible trade, alliances,
raids, migrations, and disasters. Off-screen change must have inspectable
causes and material outcomes.

### Deliberation matters more than reflex

The Chronicle runs on a pausable fixed clock. No meaningful decision depends
on input speed. Danger can be severe, but the game rewards understanding,
preparation, and strange solutions rather than execution tests.

### Power scales through consequence

Early Expressions move a body or a stone. Mature Expressions may redirect
Agents, open Strata, alter settlements, or participate in world-scale change.
Growth should expand what the player can mean and affect, not merely increase a
damage number.

## The five connected loops

### Discovery

Explore → encounter something strange → see the words it plausibly embodies →
choose what to Study → gain Understanding → keep the learned word in the Codex.

### Build

Choose from the Codex → configure a bounded Loadout → form Expressions → use
them to change the Chronicle.

### Death and rebirth

Lose a body → preserve the Chronicle and Codex → inhabit a new Incarnation →
make a fresh build from the language already learned.

### Settlement

Establish a Home → attract or recruit Agents → communicate intent → make,
trade, build, and defend → establish further Holdings → accumulate material
history.

### World

Discover a Palimpsest → confront a Chronicle-specific World Claim → inscribe
a Decree, take its power, or face another world-scale choice → permanently
change what the world can become.

These loops are not separate modes. A discovered Verb can open a route to a
rare material; that material can make a structure; the structure can change a
factional Pressure; a death during the resulting raid can force a new Loadout;
the replacement Incarnation then inherits the consequences.

## Home and expedition

The main rhythm runs outward from and back toward Home:

Home → choose an expedition → cross generated territory → delve, fight, trade,
or Study → return with words, materials, relationships, and consequences →
change Home → face the new opportunities and Pressures that change creates.

Home is the player's fortress, base, refuge, archive, and remembered address.
It can be built on a surface, in the sky, beneath a mountain, deep underground,
or in a stranger World. Its form comes from the place, materials, inhabitants,
and history rather than from a universal base template.

Home is an anchor, not a compulsory management game. A player may devote most
of a Chronicle to construction, production, Agents, and defense. Another may
keep a modest refuge and spend nearly all their time finding generated ruins,
dungeons, artifacts, creatures, and remote Worlds. Both are complete ways to
play because both return discoveries and consequences to the same Chronicle.

Returning is initially physical. Known routes can remain long and dangerous.
Later Verbs and Expressions may shorten, fold, or replace the journey,
eventually allowing true return to Home from extraordinary distances. The game
does not grant an unexplained universal fast-travel button.

## The shape of a Chronicle

The hour counts below are a scale sketch, not a pacing contract.

### The first half-hour

The player states an Intent, receives a startling First Verb, and sees a First
Horizon that was inaccessible moments earlier. They encounter at least one
phenomenon worth studying and make one persistent change. The opening proves
that the world contains rules the player did not arrive knowing.

### Around ten hours

The Codex supports several genuinely different Loadouts. The player has crossed
multiple Strata, remembers particular Landmarks, and may have survived more
than one Incarnation. A first Home gives discoveries somewhere material to
accumulate. Agents, tools, weapons, armor, artifacts, structures, and crafted
objects begin connecting exploration to settlement rather than forming a
detached recipe treadmill.

### Around fifty hours

The Chronicle has recognizable political and ecological history. Holdings form
a vulnerable network across distinct environments. Named Agents have loyalties
and grievances. Factions trade, ally, fracture, and raid for observable
reasons. The player can influence these events, but cannot reduce them to
perfectly controlled units or a spreadsheet economy.

Death at this scale should be painful without erasing meaning. A replacement
body inherits a rich Codex and a difficult world, but must choose a new active
language. An old solution may no longer fit the body, route, allies, or Pressure
now in front of it.

### Around two hundred hours

The Chronicle reads as a singular history: altered regions, remembered
catastrophes, dead Incarnations, surviving Agents, rare Expressions, damaged
Holdings, factional reversals, and routes that exist because the player made
them possible. World-scale ambitions become concrete rather than appearing as
a generic endgame menu.

A player may find a Palimpsest and pursue a World Claim: found a power, remake
a region, escape the Chronicle, become a catastrophe, or discover another
conclusion generated by that world's conditions. None is a mandatory victory
screen, and one Chronicle may contain several incompatible Claims.

## Home, Holdings, and making

Home may exist in a forest, mountain, sky, underworld, or stranger World. It is
the singular emotional and material center of the Chronicle even when damaged,
lost, or reduced to ruins. Additional Holdings can extend its routes, shelter
Agents, exploit discoveries, or become powers with histories of their own.
None are interchangeable base slots.

Making is grounded in Chronicle matter. Resources become tools, weapons,
armor, structures, and artifacts whose provenance and loss can matter. The
goal is not a universal crafting checklist; it is to let discovery and material
history change what a Holding and its people can do.

The player gives Agents intent through capabilities such as Command. Agents
remain responsible for how they understand and act on it. A successful Holding
therefore produces stories about particular people under pressure, not an
optimal row of anonymous job assignments.

Agent is the systemic category, not a species. A helper may be a person,
mutant, beast, construct, or stranger; what matters is that it has identity and
agency. A purely mechanical tool is not an Agent, while an autonomous construct
with memory and relationships may be.

## Worlds beyond the first

A Chronicle is not limited to one surface with a sky and underworld. Each World
is a generated realm with linked Strata and its own geography, factions,
ecologies, resources, Landmarks, and history. The Chronicle can expand without
an authored edge as the player reaches farther territory and stranger Worlds.

Vertical travel is qualitative, not merely a larger coordinate. `Fly` may cross
between a surface and its nearby sky; `Soar` might sustain travel into heights
where another World or passage becomes reachable. Burrowing may open an
underworld, while a rarer deep-travel Verb can cross a lower frontier that
ordinary digging cannot.

Using a Verb repeatedly should create opportunities rather than a grind meter.
Flying into difficult heights may expose phenomena, creatures, artifacts, or
tomes that offer `Soar`; holding the Fly input thousands of times should not
automatically award it. Discovery remains the source of new language.

The Chronicle is unbounded in reach, not simulated everywhere at full detail.
Generated but untouched territory waits deterministically. Known distant
places accumulate causal history at an appropriate resolution, while the area
around the current Incarnation receives full attention.

## Palimpsests

Palimpsests are discovered, world-scale artifacts associated with the
possibility of permanently changing a Chronicle's rules while leaving its prior
history visible. They are extraordinarily rare. A generated World may contain
none, one, or two, but never more.

Their exact affordances remain intentionally unsettled. One strong direction is
to let a player inscribe a Decree: a bounded, deterministic, legible rule
change whose scope, cost, and consequences arise from the Chronicle. Another is
to steal or redirect the artifact's power toward the current self, faction,
Home, World, or catastrophe.

Whatever the final interaction becomes, it must produce durable history rather
than arbitrary prose, a prompt, or code written by the player. A Palimpsest is
an apex possibility, not a compulsory final boss, a guaranteed discovery, or
the only valid reason to continue a Chronicle.

## Visual identity

The world is shown as an orthographic, grid-first, symbolic pixel space between
pure ASCII and fully illustrated tiles. Limited palettes, strong silhouettes,
layered marks, and adjacency-aware terrain make dense information readable at
a glance. Settlements and interiors use the same visual language as the
wilderness rather than becoming separate presentation modes.

Visual Grammar makes generated places feel related without making them
identical. Actors, materials, structures, passages, and Landmarks must remain
legible before decorative detail. The interface is dark, restrained, and
secondary to the map; the world should read like a Chronicle, not a dashboard.

## Influences, not templates

- **Caves of Qud** contributes alien discovery, systemic possibility, and
  bodies that can become strange.
- **Dwarf Fortress** contributes persistent history, material consequence, and
  a world that continues outside the camera.
- **RimWorld** contributes readable settlement pressure and stories about
  autonomous people.
- **Path of Exile** contributes the appetite for unhinged build expression and
  long-horizon possibility.

The synthesis is its own game: power words are the bridge between personal
discovery, repeated Incarnations, material settlement, and world-scale change.

## What this is not

- Not a roguelike run reset; death preserves the world and learned language.
- Not a hand-authored campaign or a set of fixed levels.
- Not unstructured procedural soup; Grammar, causality, and memory matter.
- Not a natural-language programming game or revival of the removed prototype.
- Not a conventional class tree with every learned skill active at once.
- Not a colony-control game in which people become obedient worker tokens.
- Not a crafting treadmill detached from discovery and material consequence.
- Not a reflex action game.
- Not a promise to simulate every system before a small playable slice proves
  why that system belongs.

## The product test

Every major feature should strengthen at least one of these questions without
breaking the others:

1. What did I discover that I did not know this Chronicle allowed?
2. What can I express now that I could not express before?
3. What persisted because I acted, left, or died?
4. Which person, place, faction, or material made this event happen?
5. What new ambition became imaginable because the world changed?

If a feature cannot answer one of those questions in play, it is not yet part
of this game.
